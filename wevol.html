WeVol - iOS Application Design



Timeline: 
3 months
Team:
Ritambhara Singh, Yu-Kuan Hwang, Zihan Yang
Tools:
Sketch - Design
Balsamiq - Lo-fi Prototype
Figma - Medium-fi Prototype
iMovie - Video editing
Main Responsibilities:
Design, Prototype and Presentation


Motivation:
According to study about Volunteering trend in America in 2015 by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, Volunteer rates were lowest among 20- to 24-year-olds (18.4 percent). We decided to make a mobile application as 86% of Americans in this age group own a smartphone.

System Concept Statement:
WeVol is a platform for sharing and managing information about volunteer organizations and activities.
This app is meant to serve two categories of users:

  • 1. People who want to volunteer for a cause
  • 2. People (organizers) who need volunteers for a cause.


Process:

Contextual Inquiry and Analysis:


This phase involved the following:

  • 1. Interviews
  • 2. Affinity Diagram
  • 3. Workflows


After creating an interview script, we recruitred 8 people for the "Volunteer" user profile in a pair. We wanted to know about their past experiences and expectations from a volunteering event.
Next, we created an affinity diagram based on our interview notes; categorized and recategorized notes.

The last step in this phase was to identify work roles,sub roles and machine roles and developed initial flow model.
We collected raw contextual data mainly by conducting interviews. We also acquired some basic information of organizations by talking to organizers on the UT Volunteer Fair. Basically we collected both quantitative data through questions such as “What’s the longest distance did you travel for the volunteer service? And how did you get there?” “How much time are you willing to spend on volunteer service?” and qualitative data through questions like “What kind of system did you use to apply for the activity? Can you walk through your experience with the system?” “What does a successful event look like to you as a volunteer?”

Requirement Extraction and Modeling:


In this step each of us took several categories of affinity diagram and wrote down all the key points. For each key point, we converted what they want/don’t want/like/dislike to user’s requirements. Then we collected all the requirements and removed duplicated ones.


We came up with the following models of our project:

  • 1. User Model
  • 2. Usage Model
  • 3. Task Structure Model

    • The task structure model is displayed here. A task structure of the system, not only provided us with insights to how user might use our system, but also guided the overall structure for our system as user tasks are broken down into sub-steps. Through hierarchy task inventory, we may find additional needs to tasks that need to be addressed, as each step is critical in order to reach the goal of the user.


    Task structure model for the Volunteer class


Design:


We started our sketch based on the primary persona we came up with, which emcompasses the main goals of sub-roles. Then we came up with possible features we concluded in the ideation, including what the users will see when they first installed the app, the home page, the events, how to apply for those events, feedback, etc. We ended with a rough sketch of what our system might look like.

Prototype:


We initially did a lo-fi prototype on Balsamiq. Building a lo-fi prototype helped in externalzing our ideas in less time and effort. It included factors we gathered from the interviews that were important. For the low-fi prototype, we used the the “T” prototype, to try to include different features while at the same time try to add depth to some parts.


After doing three usability tests on the low-fi prototype, we developed our medium-fi prototype based on the feedbacks in Figma.


Lo-fi wireframe screenshot from Balsamiq

      

Click here to download the low-fi Balsamiq Prototype file.
For the medium-fi prototype, we focused more on the depth rather than the features. As feedback from the pilot test indicated that we originally had too much information on screen. Thus, downsizing of the features led to cleaner and simpler interface.
We implemented iOS design guidelines to ensure that our UI elements are consistent for users.


Try out the Medium Fidelity Prototype


Evaluation and Recommendation:


Overall our prototype allows users to search, view, register for and cancel volunteer activities. From the user test we noticed that wording and error prevention are the main concerns of WeVol.


Lessons learned


Overall, one major part of this project was documentation. I realized that regular consolidation of raw data and documentation of the progress was extremely helpful. As there was a need of constantly referring to previous phases, it was useful to have notes from those phases. I will try to practice this in all my upcoming UX projects. Another major aspect of this project was working in a team. I found working on UX projects in a team exceedingly helpful. It helped me get rid of my mind’s bias and offered me more than one perspective. As this was my first UX project, I was figuring things out before implementing different steps of the UX cycle. For upcoming projects, I will try to implement best practices of UX.
